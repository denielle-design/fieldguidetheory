Geology & Soils of the Lowveld
NQF2 coreweek 1quiz

Geology & Soils of the Lowveld

The geology of the Greater Kruger determines everything. Why certain trees grow where they do, which animals prefer which soils, how water moves through the landscape. Get this right and every interpretation you give as a guide has a foundation under it.

The Lowveld sits on ancient granite and basalt. Granitic soils are sandy, nutrient-poor and drain quickly. Basalt soils are heavy, clay-rich and fertile. Each supports a completely different community of plants and animals.

Key concepts

  • Granite vs basalt: formation, texture, colour, drainage
  • Soil horizons and how weathering creates them
  • How soil type determines vegetation type (sweet vs sour veld)
  • What rock outcrops, drainage lines and soil colour tell you about the landscape
  • Dolerite intrusions in the Lowveld and their ecological significance

Why it matters on drives

You can explain to guests why elephants congregate along drainage lines on basalt in the dry season, or why the mopane belt is so uniform. It is the geology. Not coincidence.

Weather, Climate & Lowveld Seasons
NQF2 coreweek 1quiz

Weather, Climate & Lowveld Seasons

The Lowveld runs on two seasons: hot, wet summer (Oct–Mar) and warm, dry winter (Apr–Sep). What you see on drive, when you go out, what the animals are doing — all of it is shaped by this rhythm.

Dry season is generally better for game viewing. Animals concentrate around water and the vegetation is low. The wet season brings lush green, newborns, and spectacular birdlife — but game is harder to spot because everything is spread out and hidden.

Key concepts

  • The Intertropical Convergence Zone (ITCZ) and SA rainfall patterns
  • Berg winds, thunderstorm formation, afternoon build-up in summer
  • Reading cumulus clouds for afternoon storms on drive
  • Temperature range across seasons — Hoedspruit can hit 45°C in October
  • How seasonal change affects animal behaviour, plant phenology, and water availability

Astronomy & the Southern Night Sky

Astronomy & the Southern Night Sky

Sundowners and sleep-outs under a Limpopo sky are some of the most powerful experiences you can give guests. Being able to narrate that sky — from the Southern Cross to the Milky Way core — is a signature guiding skill. Learn it before you arrive.

To find south using the Southern Cross: draw a line from the top to the bottom star of the cross, extend it 4.5 times that length, and you reach the South Celestial Pole. The Pointer Stars (Alpha and Beta Centauri) confirm you have the right cross.

Key concepts

  • Finding south using the Southern Cross and the Pointer Stars
  • Milky Way core visibility: best from the Lowveld May to September
  • Key constellations: Scorpius, Orion, Sagittarius, Centaurus
  • Planets vs stars: planets do not twinkle
  • Moon phases and their effect on nocturnal animal behaviour
  • Traditional African star lore for storytelling context

Study resources

Ecology: How Ecosystems Function
NQF2 coreweek 2quiz

Ecology: How Ecosystems Function

Ecology is the study of relationships — between organisms and their environment, between predator and prey, between fire and vegetation. It is the framework that holds all other guiding knowledge together. Without it, you are just listing animals.

A change in lion numbers affects herbivore behaviour, which affects vegetation, which affects water retention, which affects invertebrates. The whole system ripples. This is what ecological literacy actually means — seeing those connections in real time.

Key concepts

  • Trophic levels: producers, primary consumers, secondary consumers, decomposers
  • Food webs vs food chains: webs are more accurate because nothing eats just one thing
  • Nutrient cycling: nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon cycles in savanna
  • Carrying capacity and its limiting factors: food, water, habitat, disease
  • Keystone species and ecosystem engineers: elephant, termites, fig trees
  • Ecological succession: from bare soil to climax community
  • Fire in savanna: regulation, not destruction

Study resources

Biomes of Southern Africa
NQF2 coreweek 3quiz

Biomes of Southern Africa

Southern Africa has nine recognised biomes. Your campus sits in the Savanna — specifically the Mopane-Bushwillow Woodland subtype of the Lowveld. Knowing the others gives you context for why the Savanna works the way it does.

South Africa has more plant species than the entire Northern Hemisphere. The Fynbos biome alone contains over 9,000 plant species, 70% found nowhere else on Earth. It is one of the world's six floral kingdoms and one of its most threatened.

The nine biomes

  • Savanna: trees and grass, fire-driven, home to the Big Five
  • Fynbos: SW Cape, extraordinarily diverse, fire-adapted shrubland
  • Grassland: highveld, dominated by grasses, many endemics
  • Succulent Karoo: arid W Cape, highest succulent diversity on Earth
  • Nama-Karoo: semi-arid central plateau
  • Thicket: dense woody vegetation, Eastern Cape
  • Forest: small patches at Knysna, Tsitsikamma, Drakensberg
  • Albany Thicket: shrubby, eastern Cape
  • Desert: Namib, narrow coastal strip along the west

Taxonomy & Classification
NQF2 coreweek 3quiz

Taxonomy & Classification

Taxonomy is the science of naming and classifying living things. Understanding the hierarchy from Kingdom down to Species helps you explain relationships between organisms and use scientific names with confidence rather than as decoration.

Carl Linnaeus invented the binomial naming system in 1758. Scientific names are always italicised: Panthera leo (lion), Loxodonta africana (African elephant). They are universal across every language on Earth — worth explaining to guests.

The hierarchy

  • Domain, Kingdom, Phylum, Class, Order, Family, Genus, Species
  • Mnemonic: Dear King Philip Came Over For Good Soup
  • Practice on the Big 5 first — know their full taxonomic classification

Key families for the Lowveld

  • Felidae (cats), Canidae (dogs and jackals), Bovidae (antelope and buffalo)
  • Accipitridae (eagles, hawks, vultures), Bucerotidae (hornbills)
  • Fabaceae (acacias, mopane), Combretaceae (bushwillows)

Lowveld Trees & Botany
NQF2 coreweek 4quiz

Lowveld Trees & Botany

Plants are the foundation of every ecosystem. Botany is the subject most students underestimate. Being able to name and interpret at least 40 to 50 common Lowveld trees and shrubs is a core NQF2 competency, and it is what separates good guides from genuinely great ones.

The Mopane tree (Colophospermum mopane) folds its butterfly-shaped leaves along the midrib in extreme heat to reduce water loss. You can see it happening. That kind of visible, explainable adaptation makes guests stop and pay attention.

Priority trees to learn first

  • Marula (Sclerocarya birrea): fruit, elephant association, traditional beer
  • Mopane (Colophospermum mopane): heat adaptation, mopane worm harvest
  • Leadwood (Combretum imberbe): hardest wood in Africa, distinctive white bark
  • Sycamore Fig (Ficus sycomorus): keystone species, produces figs year-round
  • Jackalberry (Diospyros mespiliformis): grows on termite mounds, edible fruit
  • Knob Thorn (Senegalia nigrescens): giraffe's preferred food tree
  • Apple Leaf (Philenoptera violacea): early leaf flush, stunning purple flowers

Arthropods & Invertebrates
NQF2 coreweek 4quiz

Arthropods & Invertebrates

Arthropods make up over 80% of all animal species. They are routinely skipped on game drives, which is a mistake. The best guides use them to tell some of the most surprising stories on the vehicle.

The dung beetle (Scarabaeus satyrus) is the only animal besides humans known to navigate using the Milky Way. On moonless nights, they roll their dung balls in straight lines by orienting to the galactic band overhead. Tell that story once and guests never look at dung beetles the same way.

Key groups

  • Termites: ecosystem engineers; their mounds create biodiversity hotspots across the veld
  • Dung beetles: nutrient recyclers, galactic navigation, significant in ancient Egyptian mythology
  • Mantids: ambush predators, central figure in San mythology
  • Scorpions: nocturnal, fluoresce under UV light, two distinct venom types
  • Mopane worms: Emperor moth caterpillars, harvested as food, seasonal

Reptiles of the Lowveld

Reptile handling is a formal on-campus component. Knowing species ID, venom type, and safe handling technique before you arrive makes a real difference — not just to your anxiety levels, but to the guest safety protocols you will be tested on.

South Africa's most dangerous snake is the Black Mamba (Dendroaspis polylepis). Not because it has the most potent venom — the Boomslang does — but because of its speed, size, and aggressive defensive behaviour. It can move at up to 20 km/h.

Priority snakes

  • Puff Adder (Bitis arietans): responsible for most SA snakebite deaths; slow, cryptic, ambush predator
  • Black Mamba (Dendroaspis polylepis): neurotoxic, extremely fast, coffin-shaped head
  • Mozambique Spitting Cobra (Naja mossambica): cytotoxic, spits accurately at eyes
  • Boomslang (Dispholidus typus): highly venomous, rear-fanged, arboreal
  • African Rock Python (Python sebae): non-venomous constrictor, largest snake in SA

Other reptiles

  • Nile Crocodile: thermoregulation, hunting strategy, basking behaviour
  • Nile Monitor: intelligent scavenger, usually near water
  • Leopard Tortoise: largest SA tortoise
  • Flap-necked Chameleon: most common in the Lowveld, good for myth-busting

Birds of the Greater Kruger

Kruger and the greater Lowveld are home to over 500 bird species. You do not need to know them all. Know the 60 to 80 most common plus the iconic and diagnostic species, and you will have core NQF2 covered and guests genuinely impressed.

The Southern Ground Hornbill (Bucorvus leadbeateri) is one of Africa's most endangered birds. It lives in family groups, has a lifespan of over 60 years, and nests only once every nine years on average. When you see one, there is a whole story there.

Priority groups

  • Raptors: African Fish Eagle, Bateleur, Martial Eagle, Lappet-faced Vulture, White-backed Vulture, Secretary Bird
  • Hornbills: Southern Ground Hornbill, African Grey, Southern Yellow-billed, Trumpeter
  • Rollers and Kingfishers: Lilac-breasted Roller, Giant Kingfisher, Woodland Kingfisher
  • Waterbirds: Saddle-billed Stork, Goliath Heron, African Jacana
  • Nightjars and Owls: Giant Eagle Owl, Fiery-necked Nightjar — learn the call before you go

Mammals: The Big Five & Beyond
NQF2 coreweek 7quiz

Mammals: The Big Five & Beyond

The Big Five were named by hunters for difficulty on foot — not ecological importance. You need to know them deeply, plus a wider cast of ungulates, primates, smaller carnivores, and the nocturnal species guests rarely expect.

The term Big Five comes from big-game hunting. These were the most dangerous animals to track on foot. Today they are the most sought-after sightings — not because of danger, but because of their symbolic power. Worth explaining that shift to guests.

The Big Five

  • Lion (Panthera leo): cooperative hunting, pride structure, dawn and dusk active
  • Leopard (Panthera pardus): most adaptable big cat, solitary, nocturnal, caches kills in trees
  • African Elephant (Loxodonta africana): matriarchal, extraordinary memory, 300 kg of food per day
  • White Rhino (Ceratotherium simum): near-threatened, square lip, grazer, relatively social
  • Black Rhino (Diceros bicornis): critically endangered, hook lip, browser, solitary, more aggressive
  • Cape Buffalo (Syncerus caffer): dangerous when injured; solitary old bulls known as dagga boys

Animal Behaviour & Ethology

Animal Behaviour & Ethology

Ethology is what transforms a guide from a spotter into a storyteller. Being able to interpret what you are watching in real time and explain it while it is happening — that is the mark of an exceptional guide.

Impala (Aepyceros melampus) have a black gland on each hind leg that releases a pheromone for territory marking. The same gland makes a clicking sound during flight, possibly as a contact call. One small observation. A whole story.

Key behaviour categories

  • Territorial behaviour: scent marking, calling, visual displays, fighting
  • Communication: visual, olfactory, auditory, tactile
  • Alarm calls: impala, baboon, vervet, francolin alarms indicate different predator types and locations
  • Feeding strategies: browsers vs grazers, mixed feeders, optimal foraging theory
  • Mating systems: monogamy, polygyny, polyandry, seasonal breeding cues
  • Social structures: herd defence, coalitions, kin selection

Study resources

Track & Sign (CyberTracker)

Tracking is one of the oldest human skills on Earth. CyberTracker Track and Sign is a formal qualification you will work towards on campus. In guiding, it lets you reconstruct an animal's story from nothing more than pressed sand and broken twigs.

A single set of tracks in soft sand can tell you species, direction of travel, approximate time since passing, gait, size, sex, and whether the animal was relaxed or stressed. A whole story in silence.

Start learning now

  • Track morphology: toe number, claw presence or absence, pad shape
  • Gait patterns: walking, trotting, bounding, galloping — how the spacing changes
  • Age assessment: sharp edges and visible moisture mean fresh
  • Scat: colour, content (hair, bone, seeds), shape
  • Other signs: scrapes, rubs, kills, feeding sign, territorial marks

Study resources

  • CyberTracker.org: the official tracking body
  • Animal Tracks of Southern Africa — Louis Liebenberg (the classic text)
  • YouTube: Track and Sign demonstrations

Ethical Guiding & Guest Experience

Bushwise dedicates a full module and IFGA assessment to ethical guiding because unethical practices — crowding animals, chasing wildlife, tip-driven decision-making — actively harm wildlife and wreck the industry's reputation. This is assessed.

The IFGA standards are the only fully guest-centred criteria in the field guide industry. They evaluate guides on how they design and deliver experiences — not just what they know. Bushwise is the exclusive Southern African IFGA training provider.

Core ethical principles

  • Animal welfare first: distance protocol, engine off at sightings, reading stress signals
  • Saying "I don't know" is professional. Guests respect honesty more than confident guessing.
  • Minimising impact on terrain, vegetation, and other vehicles
  • Guest safety: seating protocol, briefings, decision-making under pressure
  • Radio etiquette and sighting-sharing protocol with other guides

Interpretation and storytelling

  • Tilden's Principles of Interpretation: the foundational framework
  • Reading your audience and matching content to guest interests
  • Linking the specific to the universal — why a single dung beetle connects to the whole ecosystem

Dangerous Animals & Wildlife First Aid
first aid certsafetyquiz

Dangerous Animals & Wildlife First Aid

Basic Wilderness First Aid (Unit Standard 119567) is a formal certificate you earn on campus. Understanding dangerous animal behaviour is inseparable from first aid — the best treatment is prevention, and prevention comes from reading the animal correctly.

An elephant mock charge comes with ears spread, head raised, and plenty of noise. A real charge is the opposite: head down, ears back, silent and deliberate. Knowing the difference could save a life on a walking trail.

Dangerous animal protocols

  • Elephant: read mock vs real charge; never run; use the vehicle or a tree deliberately
  • Buffalo: unpredictable when injured; dagga boys are the most dangerous
  • Hippo: more African deaths than any other large mammal; never position yourself between a hippo and water
  • Snake: stillness, slow retreat, no tourniquets, get to hospital
  • Crocodile: never approach water's edge without scanning; strike range is explosive

First aid concepts

  • BLS and CPR: airway, breathing, circulation in that order
  • Snakebite: pressure-immobilisation for neurotoxic bites (mamba, cobra); NOT for cytotoxic bites
  • Heat stroke: recognise fast, cool rapidly, evacuate
  • Field evacuation: radio communication, landing zones, briefing medics

Ethical Conservation Storytelling

Conservation storytelling turns scientific complexity into emotional connection — without distorting, sensationalising, or exploiting. In a world drowning in clickbait wildlife content, ethical storytelling is both rare and genuinely powerful.

The most effective conservation messaging does not lead with doom. Research shows that hope, agency, and specific positive outcomes motivate action far more effectively than fear or guilt. The constant extinction narrative causes compassion fatigue. Lead with what is working.

Core principles

  • Accuracy over drama: know the difference between compelling and misleading
  • Do not anthropomorphise: narrate behaviour honestly, not as a Disney film
  • Community consent and cultural sensitivity: whose story is it to tell?
  • Credit researchers and partners: conservation is collaborative work
  • Tell stories of solutions, not just problems

Accounts worth studying before you arrive

  • @africageographic: editorial, ethical, no clickbait
  • @wilddog_conservation: raw, honest field reporting
  • @charliehamiltonjames: BBC photojournalist who publishes his own ethics manifesto

Wildlife Photography Fundamentals

Wildlife Photography Fundamentals

Wildlife photography is a formal workshop component. You do not need professional gear — but you should know the exposure triangle fluently before you arrive, so on campus you can focus on composition and storytelling rather than fumbling with settings.

For wildlife, the two settings that matter most are shutter speed (freeze motion — aim for at least 1/1000s on moving animals) and continuous autofocus. Get those two instinctive. Everything else is secondary.

The exposure triangle

  • ISO: sensor sensitivity; higher ISO means brighter but noisier image
  • Aperture (f-stop): depth of field; f/5.6 on a telephoto gives beautiful background separation
  • Shutter speed: freezes or blurs motion; minimum rule is 1 divided by focal length

Wildlife-specific skills

  • Golden hour: the first hour after sunrise and last before sunset
  • Eye focus and eye-level shooting: the difference between a record shot and a portrait
  • Burst mode and pre-focusing for fast action
  • Ethical photography: never pressure a guide to approach closer for a shot

Study resources

  • Marcel van Oosten: African wildlife photography tutorials

Social Media & Content Strategy for Conservation

Conservation organisations live and die by public awareness and donations. That public attention now lives on social media. This module builds your ability to create, schedule, and analyse content that grows genuine community around conservation projects — not just followers.

Wildlife Videography & Narrative

Video is the dominant format for conservation communication. This module covers both the craft of filming in the field and the narrative structure that makes wildlife footage worth watching rather than just pretty to look at.

The most effective wildlife films follow a 3-act structure: establish the character (the animal, its world), introduce tension (a challenge, a hunt, a drought), then resolve with outcome and meaning. This applies equally to a 60-second Reel and a 60-minute documentary.

Filming fundamentals

  • Steady shots: window mounts, beanbags, breathing technique on handheld
  • Shot types: wide establishing, medium, tight detail, reaction shots — you need all four
  • Audio: ambient sound tells as much as the image; record separately when possible
  • The 180-degree rule: break it and your edit will feel wrong

Post-production

  • CapCut on your phone or DaVinci Resolve on desktop (both free)
  • Colour grading for golden hour warmth
  • Captions for accessibility and reach
  • File naming and backup workflow: critical in the field where you generate gigabytes daily

Wildlife Population Management
conservationmanagementquiz

Wildlife Population Management

Conservation is not passive. It requires active decisions about populations. Should an overpopulated elephant herd be culled, contracepted, or translocated? Each option has profound ecological, ethical, and political dimensions.

When Kruger's culling moratorium came into effect in 1994, elephant numbers grew from around 7,500 to over 20,000. That growth has transformed vegetation structure across large parts of the park. One management decision. Ecosystem-wide consequences.

Key interventions

  • Translocation: moving animals between reserves; critical for metapopulation management and genetic diversity
  • Contraception: PZP immunocontraception in elephant; effective but labour-intensive
  • Culling: ethically contentious, ecologically rational in some scenarios, politically radioactive
  • Rewilding: reintroducing locally extinct species — wild dog, cheetah, and lion in SA
  • Population monitoring: aerial surveys, mark-recapture, camera trapping

Human-Wildlife Conflict & Community Conservation

Human-Wildlife Conflict & Community Conservation

Wildlife does not recognise fences. When elephants raid crops or lions take livestock, the cost falls on local communities — often the same communities with the deepest knowledge of and connection to that wildlife. Getting this relationship wrong is how conservation fails.

Communities adjacent to conservation areas support wildlife far more strongly when they receive tangible benefits — employment, revenue sharing, education. Conservation that excludes local communities almost always fails long-term. Namibia's communal conservancy model is the clearest proof of this.

Key topics

  • Types of conflict: crop raiding (elephant), livestock predation (lion, leopard, cheetah, wild dog), human injury and death
  • Mitigation tools: chilli barriers, guardian dogs, beehive fences, reinforced bomas
  • Community-based natural resource management (CBNRM)
  • Land restitution in SA and its implications for conservation areas
  • The Karongwe placement: Rhino Revolution, EarthRanger, and community education in practice

Study resources

The Big 5 Threats to Biodiversity
conservationthreatsquiz

The Big 5 Threats to Biodiversity

The Bushwise course reframes the "Big Five" to mean the five major drivers of biodiversity loss. It is a deliberate and effective teaching move — and it puts conservation threats at the centre of how you think about the landscape from day one.

We are in the Sixth Mass Extinction — the first caused by a single species. Extinction rates are currently estimated at 100 to 1,000 times the natural background rate. South Africa is one of the world's 17 megadiverse countries, and also one of the most ecologically threatened.

HIPPO: the five threats

  • H: Habitat loss and fragmentation — agriculture, urbanisation, infrastructure; the single biggest driver globally
  • I: Invasive species — lantana, eucalyptus, water hyacinth, feral cats; they displace natives without natural limits
  • P: Pollution — agricultural runoff, plastics, light and noise pollution affecting behaviour
  • P: Poaching and overexploitation — rhino horn, elephant ivory, abalone, cycads

Field Research & Data Collection

Field research is how conservation decisions get made. You will learn to collect, record, and interpret data — skills directly applicable to placements at LEO Africa, Siyafunda, and Karongwe.

A single camera trap network can generate months of behavioural data on elusive species like leopard and aardvark at a fraction of the cost of direct observation — and without any human disturbance affecting the behaviour being recorded.

South African Conservation Law & Governance

South Africa has a sophisticated legal framework for biodiversity — and understanding it is non-negotiable for anyone serious about working in the industry. From permits required to guide to legislation governing threatened species, it all underpins the work.

NEMBA — the National Environmental Management: Biodiversity Act of 2004 — is the primary biodiversity legislation in SA. It governs threatened species, bioprospecting, alien and invasive species, and the establishment of bioregions. You will encounter it repeatedly in conservation training.

Key legislation

  • NEMA (1998): the overarching environmental management framework
  • NEMBA (2004): biodiversity, threatened species, invasive alien species
  • NEM:PAA: protected areas — national parks, nature reserves, marine protected areas
  • CITES: international trade in endangered species; SA's role in ivory and rhino horn debates
  • SANParks, CapeNature, Ezemvelo KZN Wildlife: the provincial conservation authorities

Qualifications and professional bodies

  • CATHSSETA: the SETA responsible for field guide qualifications in SA
  • FGASA (Field Guides Association of Southern Africa): the professional body

Historical Human Habitation & Cultural Context

The Lowveld has been inhabited for hundreds of thousands of years. Understanding the archaeological, cultural, and political history of this landscape — including the contested history of conservation itself — makes you a more honest and contextually grounded guide.

The establishment of Kruger National Park in 1926 involved the forced removal of multiple indigenous communities. The Makuleke Land Claim of 1998 set a critical precedent for community land rights within protected areas in SA. Modern conservation cannot be understood without knowing this.

Key topics

  • Rock art of the San: spiritual significance and distribution across SA
  • Iron Age settlements in the Lowveld and the Great Zimbabwe connection
  • Voortrekker and colonial history of Limpopo and Mpumalanga
  • Apartheid's impact on land and conservation: forced removals, homeland boundaries
  • Post-1994 land restitution and its ongoing implications for reserves
  • The Tsonga/Shangaan people: history, culture, and deep connection to the Greater Kruger landscape
